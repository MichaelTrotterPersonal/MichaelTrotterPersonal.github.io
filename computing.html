Crosswind and Daylight Calculator

This crosswind and daylight flying times calculator was created for my 'something original' part of COMP6780 Assignment 3.

The calculator is implemented entirely in JavaScript and performs two functions: providing the user with earliest/latest daylight flying times, and doing runway selection and crosswind calculations.

On loading, it will prompt to use your location. It uses this to form a GET request which it sends to a sunrise/sunset API. It then parses the results for start/end of civil twilight at your location, converts from UTC to local time, and reports these as the first/last light. These times are of significance as they mark the start/end of daytime visual flight rules based on CASA regulations.

It also accepts user inputs for runways, wind direction, and wind speed. As these are provided it plots the inputs, selects the best runway with the minimum cross-wind, and calculates the crosswind and headwind components.

Better Ski Maps

Have you ever gone to a ski resort you weren't familiar with and sat staring at the list of lift statuses and the trail map side-by-side trying to figure out which areas were open and which weren't?

When Mick moved to Canberra in 2017 he found himself frustrated that Perisher didn't display their lift status reports on top of their trail map, so he wrote a Python script to solve that problem and has now deployed it as a web app.

The app scrapes the Perisher lift status page and colour-codes each status onto their trail map. Try it out here: Better Lift Status Maps

Better LiveATC Access

LiveATC is an online repository of Air Traffic Control and pilot radio broadcast recordings.

LiveATC is an incredible asset for anyone learning to fly or just interested in aviation. However, it is also full of large periods of silence.

In early 2022 Mick wrote a Python package called PyLiveATC that allows users to programmatically access LiveATC recordings, download them, and remove the silence from them to make for better listening.
